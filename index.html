GUI Testing Arena

A Unified Benchmark for Advancing Autonomous GUI Testing Agent

Anonymous Author(s)
Dataset Tool Code

Abstract

Recent advances in foundation models, particularly large multimodal language models (MLLMs), have opened new possibilities for automating complex tasks involving diverse data types. However, their application in graphical user interface (GUI) understanding and reasoning remains underexplored. To comprehensively evaluate their capability in this domain, we introduce GTArena, a unified and extensible benchmark designed to evaluate autonomous GUI testing agents built upon MLLMs.

GTArena formalizes the testing process into three core components: test intention generation, test task execution, and GUI defect detection, each targeting different aspects of multimodal understanding and interaction. To support comprehensive evaluation, we constructed a large-scale benchmark dataset comprising 1,500+ labeled GUI defects across 300+ mobile applications, including real-world apps, code-injected defect cases, and synthetically generated GUI anomalies, which cover both display and interaction issues under diverse app environments.

Benchmark results reveal that while closed-source MLLMs achieve up to 37.0% test intention coverage and 20.45% task success rate, open-source models lag significantly, with GUI defect recall often below 10%, demonstrating their limited sensitivity to fine-grained GUI inconsistencies. To address this, we introduce a reward-guided fine-tuning method based on Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO), which leverages reward signals to enhance model sensitivity to GUI defects. Our findings shed light on the limitations of current MLLMs in GUI understanding and provide actionable guidance for building more robust and domain-adapted agents.

Dataset

We present examples of various constructed GUI defects, demonstrating the feasibility of synthesizing defects through post-processing. It highlights a feasible approach to building large-scale GUI defect datasets, including both display and interaction defects.

To further demonstrate the usability of our method, we present examples of both real-world and synthetic GUI defects. Human evaluation (N=5) shows annotators achieved only 68.3% accuracy in distinguishing synthetic from real defects, confirming the realism and effectiveness of our synthetic dataset.

Key Findings:

  • In test intention generation, GPT-4o and Claude achieve the highest coverage (above 36%), with InternVL2-40B leading open-source models at 31.01%.
  • For test task execution, Claude consistently achieves top performance across all metrics (TM, EM, SR), with GPT-4o following closely.
  • In GUI defect detection, GPT-4o achieves the highest defect recall (14.00%) with balanced accuracy, while open-source models struggle with defect sensitivity.
  • A clear performance gap exists between commercial and open-source MLLMs, with GUI defect detection emerging as the most critical bottleneck.

Table 3: GUI defect detection comparison on Qwen2.5-VL.

We compare the defect detection performance of Qwen2.5-VL under three settings: the original model, supervised fine-tuning (SFT), and GRPO-based reward-guided fine-tuning.

  • The base model tends to predict "no defect" and misses most actual issues
  • SFT significantly improves defect recall, but overfits to defect patterns and fails to recognize clean GUI states
  • The GRPO-tuned model achieves a better trade-off between sensitivity and specificity, with improved overall accuracy while maintaining moderate recall on both defective and non-defective samples

This balance indicates that GRPO encourages more robust, semantics-aware reasoning by leveraging structured rewards instead of direct label supervision. In practical settings where most screens are defect-free, such balanced behavior is essential for reliable deployment.

Capability Dissociation Analysis:

To better understand which general capabilities are most relevant to GUI testing, we fine-tuned Qwen2-VL on our GUI defect dataset and evaluated the model's pre- and post-finetuning performance on the MM-Vet benchmark. This comparison reveals a distinct pattern of domain-specific capability dissociation:

  • Enhanced OCR (+0.8%) suggests text localization is fundamental to GUI understanding—likely because visual defects often manifest as text rendering anomalies
  • Minimal spatial reasoning loss (-0.3%) implies geometric relationship modeling remains crucial for defect detection
  • Significant knowledge degradation (-1.8%) reveals that domain-specific supervision overwrites rather than supplements world knowledge, indicating its dispensability

These findings suggest that GUI testing relies more on visuoperceptual grounding - such as spatial structure and UI text — than on general world knowledge or abstract reasoning.
